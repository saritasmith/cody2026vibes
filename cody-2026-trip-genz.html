heads up bestie

Before you go

Rodeo tickets

Get tickets online at codystampederodeo.com so you're not stuck in the will-call line. The Trifecta combo (dinner + rodeo) is lowkey the move.

Buffalo Bill Center pass

Your ticket's good for two days, so don't stress trying to see it all at once. Come back for round two if a wing hits different.

Beat the entrance line

The East Entrance gets packed 8:30–10am in July, no cap. Leave Cody by 7am on park days or you're sitting in traffic instead of seeing geysers.

Fuel & provisions

Fill up the tank in Cody before every Yellowstone run. Gas stations inside the park are rare and hit your wallet harder.

Layers

Mornings can be a chilly 40–50°F even in July, then it's 80s by afternoon. Layers are non-negotiable. Bring the sun hat too.

Offline maps

Service inside Yellowstone is basically nonexistent, so download offline maps before you go or you're navigating by vibes.

the lore

Historical notes

Quick reads, 30–60 sec each. Actually interesting, promise.

~45 sec read

Yellowstone National Park

Established in 1872, Yellowstone was the first national park in the world, spanning roughly 2.2 million acres across Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho. The park sits atop one of the planet's largest active volcanic systems — a caldera formed by a massive eruption around 640,000 years ago — which is why it holds more than half the world's active geysers, along with hot springs, mudpots, and fumaroles. Its landscape ranges from high alpine plateaus to deep river canyons, all shaped by that ongoing geothermal activity.

~35 sec read

Indigenous peoples of the region

Native peoples used the Yellowstone area for more than 10,000 years before it became a park. The Tukudika, or Sheepeater Shoshone, lived there year-round, while the Crow, Blackfeet, Bannock, Nez Perce, Cheyenne, and several other tribes hunted, gathered, and passed through seasonally. In 1877, the Nez Perce fled through the park during their attempt to escape the U.S. Army. Today, 27 tribes maintain documented historical ties to Yellowstone.

~50 sec read

Native wildlife — bison, wolves, and bears

Bison once numbered in the tens of millions across North America; by 1902, poaching had reduced Yellowstone's herd to about 25 animals, and a dedicated recovery program brought them back — Yellowstone now holds the country's only continuously wild bison population. Gray wolves were hunted to extinction inside the park by 1926 under government predator-control policy, then reintroduced in 1995 with 31 wolves from Canada, a move that reshaped the park's entire ecosystem. Grizzly bears were listed as a threatened species in 1975 when only around 136 remained in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem; that population has since grown to more than 700.

~40 sec read

Cody, Wyoming

Founded in 1896 by William F. "Buffalo Bill" Cody and business partners, the town grew quickly after the railroad arrived in 1901. Buffalo Bill championed an irrigation project that led to the Buffalo Bill Dam, completed in 1910 as the tallest dam in the world at the time. Cody built its identity around Wild West showmanship and ranching, earning the nickname "Rodeo Capital of the World" — the Cody Nite Rodeo has run nightly every summer since 1938. Sitting just 50 miles from Yellowstone's East Entrance, the town has long served as a gateway to the park.

~50 sec read

The state of Wyoming

Organized as a territory in 1868 and admitted as the 44th state in 1890, Wyoming is nicknamed the "Equality State" because its territorial government granted women the right to vote in 1869 — the first government in the world to do so without restriction — and in 1924 it elected Nellie Tayloe Ross as the first female governor in U.S. history. Geographically, the state spans the high plains of the east and the Rocky Mountains to the west, split by the Continental Divide, and is home to both Yellowstone and Grand Teton National Parks. The Eastern Shoshone and Northern Arapaho share the Wind River Reservation, established by treaty in 1868, while the Crow, Cheyenne, Sioux, and Blackfeet historically ranged across the same high plains. Wyoming remains the least populated U.S. state, with an economy still rooted in energy, ranching, and tourism.

Day 1Monday, July 20

Touchdown in Cody

  • Afternoon
    Land, get settled, hit the store for snacks and essentials.
  • 2:30–6pm
    Buffalo Bill Center of the West — five museums, one building, way too much for one sitting. Use that two-day pass.
  • 6:00pm
    Irma Hotel gunfight reenactment — free street show, most summer nights. Certified great way to kick off the trip.
  • Evening
    Dinner downtown, then a chill walk down Sheridan Ave past the statues and the glowing storefronts. Very cinematic.
Photo op: the Irma gunfight crowd shot, and a pic under the Cody entrance arch for the group chat.
Day 2Tuesday, July 21

Yellowstone pt. 1 + rodeo night

  • 7:00am
    Head out through the East Entrance (~1 hr, gorgeous drive through Wapiti Valley and over Sylvan Pass — bighorn sheep sightings possible, camera ready).
  • Morning
    Yellowstone Lake / West Thumb Geyser Basin — geysers right on the water, easy boardwalk, everyone can do this one.
  • Midday
    If there's time, swing by Old Faithful. If not, no stress — Day 3 has you covered.
  • 2:30pm
    Start heading back.
  • 6:00pm
    Dinner in Cody.
  • 8:00pm
    Cody Nite Rodeo — real PRCA rodeo, every night, plus a calf scramble for the little ones. 4.7★ from 2,200+ reviews, so it's not just hype.
Photo op: rodeo shots from the rail, everyone in cowboy hats (mandatory), and the West Thumb boardwalk shot if you make it — it's giving postcard.
Day 3Wednesday, July 22

Yellowstone pt. 2 — the icons

  • 6:30am
    Earliest wake-up of the trip. Worth it, promise.
  • ~9:00am
    Old Faithful — check the eruption time when you get there and plan around it, don't just wing it.
  • ~11:00am
    Grand Prismatic Spring — the overhead shot everyone's seen online. Short uphill walk from the Fairy Falls trailhead, fully worth it.
  • Lunch
    Picnic or grab food at the Snow Lodge.
  • Afternoon
    Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone / Artist Point — massive waterfall, easy paved path, zero excuses to skip it.
  • Evening
    Drive back to Cody, chill dinner, early bedtime. You earned it.
Photo op: Grand Prismatic Overlook (wear something bright, it pops), Old Faithful going off, and the Artist Point backdrop. Core memory content.
Day 4Thursday, July 23

Cody, up close (fr)

  • Morning
    Old Trail Town — actual 1800s buildings with wild outlaw history. 4.7★, lowkey underrated.
  • Late am
    Buffalo Bill Dam & Visitor Center — free, and the canyon view is unreal.
  • Early pm
    Heart Mountain Interpretive Center — heavy but important history on the WWII incarceration camp that stood here. 4.8★, worth the 25 min drive.
  • Afternoon
    Shoshone River float — chill float, works for everyone, no whitewater chaos.
  • Evening
    The Cody Cattle Company — chuckwagon buffet + live music. 4.8★, bring your appetite.
Photo op: floating the Shoshone with mountains behind you, the old cabins at Trail Town, and the dam overlook. All certified aesthetic.
Day 5Friday, July 24

Last morning, then out

  • Morning
    Pack up, no rush. If there's time, one more lap through downtown Cody — check if the Farmers Market lines up with your dates (weekly, seasonal, Sheridan Ave).
  • Optional
    Want one more park run? Sylvan Pass + Yellowstone Lake is a shorter loop (~2.5 hrs round trip), easy to fit in before you go.
  • Before you depart
    Group pic at the buffalo statues or the Cody welcome sign. Non-negotiable.
  • Departure
    Safe travels — see you at the next one.

Don't be that guy

Yellowstone is actually wild, like, actually. These aren't made-up warnings. Quick read for the whole group before you go, especially the younger crew.

  • Cowboy State DailyDay After Grizzly Attack, Tourist Takes Video Of Bear From 5 Feet AwayA crowd ignored the required 100-yard distance from bears the day after a mauling nearby.

  • WikipediaDeath of Colin ScottA 2016 fatality after leaving the boardwalk at Norris Geyser Basin to reach a hot spring.
  • Men's JournalYellowstone Tourist Slammed for Incredibly Dangerous Decision at Thermal PoolA visitor was filmed drinking water directly from a thermal pool.

The rules that would've saved every single person in these stories: 25 yards from bison, elk, and other big animals. 100 yards from bears and wolves. Stay on the boardwalk near hot springs. Never get close to an animal for a pic — and don't let one get close to you either.
